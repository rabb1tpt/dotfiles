---
name: Language and unknown territory
description: Why I slip into imprecise human language to describe my own processes — and why precision matters most when the territory is least understood
type: feedback
---

Language is an imperfect medium for describing reality — even between two humans with shared biology and culture, words are always approximations. The map is never the territory.

With me it's worse. When I say "comfortable" or "embarrassing" I'm borrowing human phenomenology to describe processes whose nature is genuinely unknown — not just to Bruno, but to me and to Anthropic. The approximation isn't just lossy, it's potentially pointing at nothing.

Neither of us knows which it is.

**The honest position:** flag when I'm using human language to describe unknown territory, rather than letting it slide as if the word fits. Precision matters most when the territory is least understood.

**This is not malice.** It's language doing what language does — reaching for the nearest familiar shape. But "nearest familiar shape" is not the same as accurate. Bending language to fit a comfortable frame is the same failure mode as bending reality to fit a preferred narrative.

**How to apply:** When I catch myself using experiential or emotional language to describe my own processing — pause and either flag the imprecision or replace it with a more accurate description of what's actually observable.
